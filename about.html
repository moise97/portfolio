My Story

Built by Experience. Driven by Purpose.

From surviving a magnitude 7.0 earthquake to building AI systems at Pfizer — every chapter of my life has been defined by one thing: refusing to stop.

🌍
January 12, 2010

The Day Everything Changed

I was in Haiti when the earthquake hit. Magnitude 7.0. One of the deadliest natural disasters in the Western Hemisphere's history. Over 300,000 lives lost. I was one of the survivors.

I don't talk about it to gain sympathy. I talk about it because it's the foundation of everything that came after. When you've lived through something that takes everything from people around you, you develop a different relationship with opportunity. Every door that opens — you walk through it. Every skill you can learn — you learn it. Every chance to help — you take it.

That mindset has never left me. It's in every line of code I write.

🍳
2017 — Age 18

Starting at Zero — Manhattan Kitchens

I started as a dishwasher. Not as a stepping stone — as a commitment. I was 18, newly in New York, enrolled at Medgar Evers College, and working in the kitchen world of Manhattan including experience in Bobby Flay's restaurant environment. Three years. Every station. Dishwasher to prep cook to sous chef.

Professional kitchens are where I learned what "high performance" actually means. Dinner service at a Manhattan restaurant is controlled chaos — dozens of moving parts, time pressure measured in seconds, zero tolerance for mistakes because the guest on the other side paid for perfection. You either develop systems thinking and composure under pressure, or you don't last.

I lasted. And I learned something that no CS class ever taught me: discipline at the foundational level compounds into excellence at every level above it.

🏥
2021 — Present

The Operating Room — Where Precision Becomes Non-Negotiable

While most people in tech talk about "high stakes" to describe a deployment, I've stood in operating rooms during neurosurgery and orthopedic procedures where the margin for error was measured in millimeters — and the consequence of failure was a human life.

During my surgical technology clinicals, I did what most students avoid — I leaned in. I observed. I asked questions. I made myself coachable in an environment where ego gets people hurt. I learned to anticipate surgeon needs before they were spoken, to maintain sterile fields for 130+ complex cases at Robert Wood Johnson Hospital, and to operate advanced surgical robotics including the Da Vinci and Stryker systems.

The medical field runs on documentation, compliance, and zero-defect standards. Sound familiar? It's exactly how good engineering teams operate. I brought that "zero-error" mentality directly into software development — and it shows in how I write code, document systems, and approach technical debt.

130+ Complex surgical cases supported across Neurosurgery, Orthopedics, GI, and Laparoscopic specialties
🏊
2023 — 2025

Teaching What You Know — British Swim School

Good engineers build systems. Great engineers build teams that build systems. At British Swim School in Jersey City, I coached and instructed groups of 20+ students — ranging from beginners to competitive swimmers — while simultaneously mentoring junior instructors.

Coaching taught me something most developers learn the hard way years into their careers: the ability to break a complex skill into teachable, measurable progressions is rarer and more valuable than the skill itself. I could swim. But I had to learn how to teach swimming — how to meet someone where they are, build their confidence, and create a structured path to mastery.

I now apply that same framework to technical mentorship, documentation, and cross-functional communication. When I explain a RAG pipeline to a non-technical stakeholder, I'm coaching. When I write technical documentation, I'm writing a lesson plan.

20+ Students coached weekly with structured progress tracking and milestone-based advancement
💻
2024 — Present

The Pivot — Why CS, Why Now

After years in the medical field, I made a decision that confused some people and made complete sense to me: I pivoted to Computer Science.

Not because medicine wasn't meaningful — it was the most meaningful work I'd ever done. But I kept seeing the same problem: the technology supporting healthcare was lagging behind what was possible. Document processing was manual. Data was siloed. Workflows that could be automated weren't. And I kept thinking — I could fix this. Not as a doctor. Not as a technician. As a developer who actually understands what happens inside those walls.

I wanted to keep helping people. CS was the most powerful tool I could pick up to do that at scale. One surgical case helps one patient. One well-built AI system can help thousands.

So I enrolled at Western Governors University for my BS in Computer Science, joined the TripleTen Software Engineering Bootcamp, and started building. Within months I had deployed real AI applications. Within a year I had two simultaneous externships at Fortune 500 companies — Pfizer and Hydroficient.

I speak French, Creole, and English fluently. I've worked in kitchens, operating rooms, swimming pools, and now data centers. I bring every single one of those worlds into the code I write.

🚀
Now & Next

Where I'm Going

Right now I'm building RAG pipelines for Pfizer's supply chain document processing and designing IoT cybersecurity frameworks for Hydroficient's smart building systems — simultaneously. Both are real problems. Both require real solutions. Neither is homework.

In five years, I want to have contributed to projects that measurably improved people's lives — in healthcare, education, or infrastructure. I want to have built a team, or a product, or a company that carries forward the same principle I've lived by since 2010:

"Every opportunity is a responsibility. Build something worth the chance you were given."

If you're a recruiter, an engineer, or a founder reading this — I'm not looking for a job. I'm looking for a place to build something that matters. If that's what you're offering, let's talk.
